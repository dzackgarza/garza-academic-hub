# What To Do as an Undergraduate

I defer to this excellent page from the math department at UC Irvine: [https://www.math.uci.edu/math-majors/math-grad-school-resources/](https://www.math.uci.edu/math-majors/math-grad-school-resources/)

# General Notes/Remarks

* Anything lower-division: [Khan Academy](https://www.khanacademy.org/). Just be sure to work the problems!
* On Youtube videos: *many* lectures and talks are being posted online these days, and these can vary in quality. This is slightly mitigated by the fact that you can watch them at 2x speed.
* On video lectures in general: they are a crutch! Use them to supplement and enrich your understanding, but passively watching a lecture is a low-utility activity when it comes to learning.
  
  The best resources are always materials from your own instructors and courses. If you do passively watch videos, it helps to engage yourself: write questions in your notes, timestamps/title of important topics, etc.
* [MIT OCW](https://ocw.mit.edu/index.htm): the video lectures (when available) are generally of very high quality. These cover a quite a few areas, and are also a good source of practice problems and exams.
* Look (or ask) for book recommendations for your topic/class on [Math StackExchange (MSE)](https://math.stackexchange.com/) or [MathOverflow (MO)](https://mathoverflow.net/). Don’t be afraid to consult multiple books on the same topic! I’ve generally had good luck following the [reference-request tags](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/tagged/reference-request).
* If you find an author/lecturer/general source that you particularly like or learn well from, see what other content they have! If they cover other topics, be open to learning those – you never quite know what will be useful where.
* For lower division or introductory courses, try to [obtain a textbook](https://lmgtfy.app/?q=libgen) paired with a solutions manual. This is a great way to drill problems, check your answers, and identify your weaknesses. Beware typos!
* Don’t bother with sites like Chegg. The solutions there are *often* incorrect, and this can lead to academic integrity issues.
* For computational or engineering courses: Schaum’s Outlines can be useful, but the quality is *very* subject-dependent.
* Learn how to use a Computer Algebra System (CAS). Don’t use it as a crutch - just learn enough syntax so that you can quickly run “sanity checks” on your computations. [Wolfram Alpha](http://www.wolframalpha.com/) and [Symbolab](https://www.symbolab.com/) are good for quick checks in lower-division courses.
* Learn something like [SageMath](http://www.sagemath.org/index.html), which can do symbolic computations and can be used for quite a bit of number theory, linear algebra, group theory, and more.

# Preparing for Graduate School

* 📺 [Introduction to Higher Mathematics](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLZzHxk_TPOStgPtqRZ6KzmkUQBQ8TSWVX)
  * A great survey that highlights many different areas of advanced mathematics.
* 📖 *Garrity*, [All the Mathematics You Missed: But Need to Know for Graduate School](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0521797071)
  * If you’re thinking about grad school at all, read this! Even if you’re not, it’s a pretty good collection of mathematics that it’s good to at least be familiar with. You can also use this to get an idea of some of the major theorems and results in a variety of subfields.

# Resources by Subject

Below are resources for specific courses/subjects; I’ve tried to roughly organize these by increasing complexity with respect to a typical undergraduate Math degree.

**Legend**
* 📖 – Textbooks
* 📺 – Videos or online lectures

## Lower Division

### Single Variable Calculus

<ul>
  <li>📖 *James Stewart*, **Calculus: Early Transcendentals**
    <ul>
      <li>The standard textbook covering the traditional curriculum. Good choice because of plentiful solution manuals and clear graphics.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Michael Spivak*, **Calculus**
    <ul>
      <li>Exposition is more advanced, leaning closer to introductory real analysis. Excellent for an honors-level course or rigorous foundation.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Tom M. Apostol*, **Calculus, Vol. 1: One-Variable Calculus, with an Introduction to Linear Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>A highly rigorous, classic alternative to Spivak, introducing topics from a historical and linear-algebraic standpoint.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 *Patrick Jones*, [**patrickJMT**](https://www.youtube.com/user/patrickJMT/videos)
    <ul>
      <li>Superb, bite-sized video tutorials walking through how to solve specific calculus and engineering computational problems.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>🔗 ⭐ *Paul Dawkins*, [**Paul's Online Notes (Calculus I & II)**](http://tutorial.math.lamar.edu/Classes/CalcI/CalcI.aspx)
    <ul>
      <li>Follows the standard curriculum very closely, offering clear, comprehensive explanations with many step-by-step examples.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Multivariable & Vector Calculus

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *H. M. Schey*, **Div, Grad, Curl, and All That: An Informal Text on Vector Calculus**
    <ul>
      <li>An absolute classic. While most textbooks introduce vector fields and operators in a highly formal way, this book focuses on physical intuition and geometric understanding.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 ⭐ *Denis Auroux*, [**MIT OCW: Multivariable Calculus**](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/mathematics/18-02sc-multivariable-calculus-fall-2010/)
    <ul>
      <li>Excellent recorded lectures featuring a highly geometric approach, great diagrams, and clear, visual breakdowns of vector mechanics.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>🔗 *Paul Dawkins*, [**Paul's Online Notes (Calculus III)**](http://tutorial.math.lamar.edu/Classes/CalcIII/CalcIII.aspx)
    <ul>
      <li>More fantastic, reliable reference notes from Paul focusing on 3D coordinates, vectors, and triple integration.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Ordinary Differential Equations

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Stephen W. Goode and Scott A. Annin*, **Differential Equations and Linear Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>Brilliant because it integrates linear algebra first, which makes understanding systems of linear differential equations and operators (e.g. eigenvalues/functions) immensely cleaner.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 *Haynes Miller / Arthur Mattuck*, [**MIT OCW: Differential Equations**](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/mathematics/18-03-differential-equations-spring-2010/index.htm)
    <ul>
      <li>A physics-motivated approach to differential equations, focusing heavily on Fourier series, mechanical systems, and linear operations.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Linear Algebra

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Sheldon Axler*, **Linear Algebra Done Right**
    <ul>
      <li>Pure mathematical gold. Axler avoids using determinants until the absolute end, showing the beauty of linear transformations, eigenvalues, and inner product spaces directly.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Stephen W. Goode and Scott A. Annin*, **Differential Equations and Linear Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>A highly concise reference that strikes a great balance between theory and computation. Excellent summaries of the invertible matrix theorem.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Howard Anton*, **Elementary Linear Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>Contains fantastic historical blurbs and an entire chapter devoted to real-world applications (computer graphics, PageRank, etc.).</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Gilbert Strang*, **Introduction to Linear Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>Strang's classic. Focuses heavily on the fundamental subspaces of a matrix and matrix factorizations (LU, QR, SVD).</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 *Gilbert Strang*, [**MIT OCW: Linear Algebra**](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/mathematics/18-06-linear-algebra-spring-2010/)
    <ul>
      <li>A legendary lecture series. Strang's teaching style and unique intuition are a must-watch.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 ⭐ *JJ*, [**JJ's Nullspace Trick**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bqBacABVCeQ)
    <ul>
      <li>An exceptionally useful shortcut for finding the nullspace basis directly from a matrix's reduced row-echelon form without solving systems by hand.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Discrete Mathematics & Proofs

<ul>
  <li>📖 *Kenneth H. Rosen*, **Discrete Mathematics and Its Applications**
    <ul>
      <li>An encyclopedic survey covering logic, sets, relations, counting, graph theory, and basic number theory.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Ralph P. Grimaldi*, **Discrete and Combinatorial Mathematics: An Applied Introduction**
    <ul>
      <li>Excellent presentation of recurrence relations, discrete math, and quantifiers.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Peter J. Eccles*, **An Introduction to Mathematical Reasoning: Numbers, Sets and Functions**
    <ul>
      <li>Perfect for students transitioning from calculus to abstract math. Great emphasis on constructing proofs.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>🔗 ⭐ *Berkeley Faculty*, [**UC Berkeley: EECS 70 (Discrete Mathematics and Probability)**](http://www.eecs70.org/)
    <ul>
      <li>Incredibly high-quality lecture notes and exercises covering proofs, induction, modular arithmetic, RSA, polynomials, and probability.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Carl M. Bender and Steven A. Orszag*, **Advanced Mathematical Methods for Scientists and Engineers**
    <ul>
      <li>A brilliant, advanced reference for asymptotic analysis, perturbation theory, and mathematical approximations.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Ronald L. Graham, Donald E. Knuth, and Oren Patashnik*, **Concrete Mathematics: A Foundation for Computer Science**
    <ul>
      <li>Covers logic, binomial coefficients, generating functions, asymptotics, and discrete structures in beautiful Knuthian detail.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

## Upper Division

### Combinatorics

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Herbert S. Wilf*, [**Generatingfunctionology**](https://www.math.upenn.edu/~wilf/DownldGF.html)
    <ul>
      <li>Freely hosted by the author. A masterclass in how to use power series to solve recurrence relations and count structures.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Miklós Bóna*, **A Walk Through Combinatorics**
    <ul>
      <li>A highly readable, engaging, and comprehensive introduction to counting, graph theory, and algorithms.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Abstract Algebra

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *David S. Dummit and Richard M. Foote*, **Abstract Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>The de-facto graduate standard but highly accessible to advanced undergraduates. Serves as an invaluable encyclopedic reference.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *John A. Beachy and William D. Blair*, **Abstract Algebra**
    <ul>
      <li>A fantastic, gentle introductory undergraduate reference focusing on integers, modular arithmetic, groups, and rings.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 ⭐ *Matthew Salomone*, [**Abstract Algebra Lecture Series**](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLL0ATV5XYF8DTGAPKRPtYa4E8rOLcw88y)
    <ul>
      <li>Incredibly well-motivated lectures. Explains structural symmetries, quotient groups, and ring theory in a highly engaging style.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 *Benedict Gross*, [**Abstract Algebra: Harvard Lectures**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VdLhQs_y_E8&list=PLelIK3uylPMGzHBuR3hLMHrYfMqWWsmx5)
    <ul>
      <li>Rigorous and crystal clear lectures from Harvard's classic abstract algebra course.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Category Theory

<ul>
  <li>📺 *Tom LaGatta*, [**What is Category Theory?**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o6L6XeNdd_k)
    <ul>
      <li>A wonderful introductory talk explaining universal properties, functors, and natural transformations. Look up Steve Awodey or Bartosz Milewski's playlists for deep-dives.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Galois Theory

<ul>
  <li>📺 ⭐ *Matthew Salomone*, [**Galois Theory Lectures**](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLL0ATV5XYF8DTGAPKRPtYa4E8rOLcw88y)
    <ul>
      <li>A modular portion of his algebra lectures. Brilliant pacing, showing the deep link between field extensions and group theory, leading up to the insolvability of the quintic.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Real Analysis

<ul>
  <li>📖 *Walter Rudin*, **Principles of Mathematical Analysis (Baby Rudin)**
    <ul>
      <li>The universal undergrad benchmark. Extremely terse and elegant. Combine with George Bergman's online commentary to make it more digestible.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>🔗 ⭐ *George Bergman*, [**Rudin Study Commentary & Exercises**](https://math.berkeley.edu/~gbergman/ug.hndts/m104_Rudin_exs.pdf)
    <ul>
      <li>Invaluable reading companions that smooth over Rudin's gaps, add intuition, and correct typos.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 ⭐ *Francis Su*, [**Real Analysis Lectures**](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PL0E754696F72137EC)
    <ul>
      <li>Su's recorded lectures from Harvey Mudd are legendary. Incredibly clear, warm, and highly motivated geometric insights.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Complex Analysis

<ul>
  <li>📖 *James Ward Brown and Ruel V. Churchill*, **Complex Variables and Applications**
    <ul>
      <li>Great undergraduate overview of residue computations, contour integrals, and conformal mapping techniques.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Tristan Needham*, **Visual Complex Analysis**
    <ul>
      <li>A work of absolute genius. Needham replaces dry calculations with breathtaking geometric explanations using Möbius transformations and vector fields.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Numerical Analysis

<ul>
  <li>📖 *Richard L. Burden and J. Douglas Faires*, **Numerical Analysis**
    <ul>
      <li>Excellent coverage of root-finding, fixed point theory, spline interpolation, and numerical differentiation/integration.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Point-Set Topology

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *James R. Munkres*, **Topology**
    <ul>
      <li>The absolute gold standard. Extremely clear, logical, and beautifully structured. The first half is point-set topology; the second is a nice introduction to algebraic topology.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *John M. Lee*, **Introduction to Smooth Manifolds (Appendix)**
    <ul>
      <li>Has a magnificent, highly-compressed appendix that reviews all the necessary point-set topology definitions and properties.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Algebraic Topology

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Allen Hatcher*, **Algebraic Topology**
    <ul>
      <li>The de-facto standard. Extremely geometric, rich in illustrations, and freely hosted online by Hatcher.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 *N. J. Wildberger*, [**Introduction to Algebraic Topology**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ap2c1dPyIVo&list=PL6763F57A61FE6FE8)
    <ul>
      <li>A wonderful undergraduate introduction to homology, triangulation, and fundamental groups.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Differential Geometry & Manifolds

<ul>
  <li>📖 *Michael Spivak*, **Calculus on Manifolds**
    <ul>
      <li>An elegant, rigorous treatment of multivariable integration, differential forms, and Stokes' theorem from a modern perspective.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📺 ⭐ *Frederic Schuller*, [**Lectures on Geometric Anatomy of Theoretical Physics**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7G4SqIboeig)
    <ul>
      <li>An absolute masterpiece. Schuller delivers the most rigorous, clean, and beautifully explained lectures on differential geometry and topology available online.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Number Theory

<ul>
  <li>📖 *William J. LeVeque*, **Fundamentals of Number Theory**
    <ul>
      <li>Very concise, classic introduction covering prime distributions, quadratic reciprocity, and Diophantine equations.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Algebraic Geometry

<ul>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Miles Reid*, **Undergraduate Algebraic Geometry**
    <ul>
      <li>Brilliant, witty, and deeply intuitive. Weaves in rich historical context and focuses on geometric examples rather than heavy algebra.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *David A. Cox, John Little, and Donal O'Shea*, **Ideals, Varieties, and Algorithms**
    <ul>
      <li>An incredible introduction using computational tools. Focuses on Gröbner bases, elimination theory, and algebraic curves.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *David A. Cox, John Little, and Donal O'Shea*, **Using Algebraic Geometry**
    <ul>
      <li>A slightly more advanced follow-up focusing on algorithms, local rings, and projective geometries.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Probability and Statistics

<ul>
  <li>📖 *Sheldon Ross*, **A First Course in Probability**
    <ul>
      <li>Excellent, problem-rich introduction to combinatorics, probability axioms, conditional probability, and distributions.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Larry Wasserman*, **All of Statistics: A Concise Course in Statistical Inference**
    <ul>
      <li>Perfect for mathematically mature students. Covers a huge amount of modern statistics and machine learning concepts concisely.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Dynamics & Chaos

<ul>
  <li>📖 *John Milnor*, **Dynamics in One Complex Variable**
    <ul>
      <li>A beautiful introduction to complex dynamical systems, Julia sets, and Mandelbrot structures.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *V. I. Arnol'd*, **Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics**
    <ul>
      <li>A mathematically advanced, deeply geometric treatment of mechanics, symplectic manifolds, and dynamics.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Steven H. Strogatz*, **Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos**
    <ul>
      <li>The best book on dynamical systems. Engagingly explains bifurcations, phase portraits, limit cycles, and strange attractors.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Jack K. Hale and Hüseyin Koçak*, **Dynamics and Bifurcations**
    <ul>
      <li>A very thorough, highly geometric and computational approach to differential equations and bifurcations.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>

### Computer Science

<ul>
  <li>📖 *Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, and Stein*, **Introduction to Algorithms (CLRS)**
    <ul>
      <li>The bible of algorithms. Incredibly rigorous, standard reference.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 ⭐ *Michael Sipser*, **Introduction to the Theory of Computation**
    <ul>
      <li>An absolute masterpiece of clear writing. Explains formal languages, automata, Turing machines, computability, and complexity theory in a highly readable format.</li>
    </ul></li>
  <li>📖 *Gayle Laakmann McDowell*, **Cracking the Coding Interview**
    <ul>
      <li>The standard resource for coding interview prep, data structures, and algorithmic puzzle-solving.</li>
    </ul></li>
</ul>
